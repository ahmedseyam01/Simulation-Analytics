Observations & Analysis

Queue Discipline (Part 4)

LCFS often produces the same average system wait (W) as FCFS mathematically, since service time is independent of order. However, individual fairness is compromised: early arrivals may be repeatedly overtaken by later arrivals, leading to starvation. Variance in wait times is significantly higher under LCFS.

Warm-up Effect (Part 5)

Without warm-up, the simulation starts with all servers idle and the first customers experience zero queue time, pulling W and Q artificially low. Excluding the first k customers allows the system to reach steady state before collecting statistics, producing more realistic estimates — especially noticeable with small n.

Multi-Server Trade-off (Part 3)

Adding servers dramatically reduces W and Q but each additional server has diminishing returns — the third server is often idle. This is the classic cost-performance trade-off: more servers increase capacity but reduce per-server efficiency. The optimal s depends on the arrival rate, service rate, and required service level.
